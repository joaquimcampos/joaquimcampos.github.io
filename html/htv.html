This Project, Simply Explained

Suppose we have a dataset \({\mathrm D}\) with \(M\) datapoints, where each datapoint is a pair of inputs and their corresponding values—mathematically, we write this statement as \({\mathrm D} = \lbrace{\mathbf x}_m, y_m\rbrace _{m=1}^M\). This dataset might be, for example, a sequence of images of mountains and their corresponding height in meters, as depicted below.

Continuing with the previous example, in supervised learning our goal is to find a model \(f\) that predicts the height \(y_m\) of each image \({\mathbf x}_m\) in our dataset fairly well [1] (i.e., \(f({\bf x}_m) = \hat{y}_m \approx y_m\)), and that is able to generalize to new inputs. Specifically, we want our model to accurately predict the height of a mountain whose image it has not seen before (i.e. is not present the training data). This is what learning is all about.

In general, this a very difficult puzzle to solve, because there are infinite models that could fit the data, and very few actually make sense. Therefore, we need to introduce some information to solve the problem. First, if we know some prior information about the data distribution, then we can use it. For example, if the data is the result of a linear mapping plus some observation noise, i.e., \(y = {\mathbf w}^T{\mathbf x} + b + \epsilon\), then we can just search within family of models and just by trying to find the optimal parameters \({\mathbf w}\) and \(b\). In this way, we transform an a priori very complicated (infinite-dimensional) problem into a very simple (finite-dimensional) one. Alternatively, we might search restrict ourselves to a family of models that we know usually works for similar tasks. Finally, we might also want to favor simple models, following the Occam's razor principle: as long as it can do the job, the simpler, the better. Simple models have the advantage of being faster to use, ocupying less memory and being more robust. Moreover, they are more explainable, since with less parameters the influence of each one in the final result becomes easier to understand. So we actually understand what is going on (unlike with ChatGPT 🤷).

Well, a family of models that has had a lot of success in the past few years are neural networks, which are within the family of piecewise-linear models 📉 [2]. The problem is that there is no way to control the number of linear regions of neural networks. In this project, we found a way to learn piecewise-linear models from the data that have few regions and a straightforward relationship with their parameters. For this, we used a regularizer that enforces sparse second-derivatives (don't mind this too much), and restricted ourselves to a family of models that is made of "lego pieces" called box splines. In the following image you can see, on the left, the lego pieces that make up our models and, on the right, the a learned model which results from summing shifted versions of this lego pieces with different heights.

We also compared our method with neural networks. The top image in this page shows, on the left, the ground-truth data from a facial dataset, on the middle, the reconstruction with our algorithm from \(M = 5000\) datapoints, and, on the right, a neural network model trained on the same data.
